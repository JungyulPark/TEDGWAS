1. Abstract
Purpose: To comprehensively characterize the molecular mechanisms underlying persistent tissue remodeling in clinically stable, inactive thyroid eye disease (TED) using high-resolution deep RNA-sequencing.
Methods: Deep bulk RNA-sequencing (approximately 250 million reads per library) was performed on orbital adipose tissue obtained from four inactive TED patients (Clinical Activity Score < 3) undergoing orbital decompression and one matched healthy control. Differential gene expression analysis and functional enrichment (GO and KEGG) were conducted. Technical replicates and Fisher’s combined p-value method were employed to ensure statistical robustness.
Results: We identified 3,200 up-regulated and 1,500 down-regulated genes in inactive TED. Up-regulated genes were significantly enriched in extracellular matrix (ECM) organization and focal adhesion, with COMP exhibiting the most dramatic increase (log2FC = 15.32). Conversely, lipid and sterol biosynthetic processes were profoundly suppressed, indicating a "metabolic shutdown" in chronic adipose tissue. The PI3K-Akt signaling pathway remained activated, suggesting sustained cell survival and remodeling signals even in the absence of clinical inflammation.
Conclusions: Clinical inactivity in TED does not equate to biological dormancy. The inactive phase is a distinct transcriptomic state characterized by robust fibrotic remodeling and metabolic exhaustion. These findings highlight a clinical-molecular disconnect and provide a molecular rationale for the efficacy of targeted IGF-1R inhibitors in chronic, clinically stable TED cases.
Keywords: Thyroid eye disease, Transcriptomics, Orbital adipose tissue, RNA-sequencing
2. Introduction
Thyroid eye disease (TED) is the most common extrathyroidal manifestation of Graves’ disease and remains a major cause of vision-threatening proptosis, diplopia and psychosocial morbidity[1, 2]. The condition affects approximately 16 per 100,000 women and 2.9 per 100,000 men annually, with a prevalence of 10 per 10,000 population[3]. First-line therapy for moderate-to-severe active TED continues to rely on high-dose intravenous glucocorticoids—with or without non-steroidal immunosuppressants—and orbital radiotherapy[2, 4, 5]. More recently, biologic agents such as tocilizumab, rituximab and the IGF-1-receptor antibody teprotumumab have entered clinical practice[5, 6]. Nevertheless, efficacy of immunosuppressive therapy has been reported to be varying from 50 to 80%[7], and treatments such as teprotumumab—which costs approximately $400,000 per treatment course—are expensive and continue to face challenges related to non-response and durability of effect[8]. These limitations underscore the need for deeper mechanistic insight and the development of better-targeted therapies for TED.
TED is an autoimmune disorder; its pathogenesis begins with loss of immune tolerance to antigens shared by thyroid and orbital fibroblasts (OFs), with autoreactive T- and B-cells infiltrating orbital connective tissue and secreting cytokines such as IFN-γ, IL-6 and TNF-α[9, 10]. These mediators stimulate resident fibroblasts to overproduce hyaluronan (glycosaminoglycans), differentiate into adipocytes or myofibroblasts, and secrete matrix-remodeling enzymes, resulting in soft-tissue expansion and fibrosis[9]. Additionally, CD34+ fibrocytes derived from circulating monocyte precursors have been shown to infiltrate the orbit and express high levels of thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR) and IGF-1R, contributing to local inflammation through the production of IL-6 and TNF-α [11, 12]. More recently, single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA‑seq) studies have begun to map this process at single‑cell resolution, revealing the importance of RASD1 expressing lipofibroblast (LPF) in TED pathogenesis and discovered the critical role of signaling molecules and its pathway such as Th17, RORγt, Th2, IL‑21R, and IFN‑γ[13-15]. Collectively, these studies have significantly elucidated the underlying cellular heterogeneity and transcriptional landscape of active TED.
Clinically, TED typically follows a biphasic course, known as Rundle’s curve, characterized by an initial active inflammatory phase followed by a chronic, clinically stable inactive phase. The Clinical Activity Score (CAS) guides therapeutic decisions, distinguishing active disease (CAS ≥3/7), which responds to immunosuppression, from inactive disease (CAS <3/7), which is traditionally managed surgically. However, the emerging clinical success of targeted biological therapies in chronic TED has fundamentally challenged the assumption that persistent proptosis and diplopia are merely irreversible structural sequelae. This observed responsiveness suggests that clinical inactivity may not necessarily equate to biological dormancy, indicating that underlying pathological processes may remain active and potentially reversible[16]. Despite this paradigm shift, while recent high-resolution studies have successfully mapped the pathogenic subsets driving active inflammation, the molecular landscape of the chronic inactive phase remains disproportionately under-investigated.
In this study, RNA-sequencing on orbital adipose tissue from four inactive TED patients undergoing orbital decompression surgery and one matched control were performed. This study aimed to comprehensively characterize the molecular mechanisms underlying persistent tissue remodeling in clinically stable TED disease and to capture global transcriptional changes associated with these changes.


3. Materials and Methods
3.1 Patient and sample information
This study included four patients with inactive TED and one control subject. TED patients were recruited from the Department of Ophthalmology between April 2021and December 2022 and met the following inclusion criteria: (1) previous diagnosis of TED with documented active phase (CAS ≥ 3/7); (2) completion of immunosuppressive treatment with clinical inactivity (CAS < 3/7) for at least 6 months; (3) indication for orbital decompression surgery due to persistent proptosis (>2 mm above normal range for ethnicity or >2 mm asymmetry between eyes), diplopia, or exposure keratopathy; and (4) euthyroid status for at least 3 months. Exclusion criteria included: (1) previous orbital surgery or radiotherapy; (2) ongoing immunosuppressive treatment; (3) other autoimmune diseases; and (4) malignancy.
All TED patients showed normal thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4), negative or low-titer thyroid antibodies (TSI, TRAb), and inactive disease status (CAS 0-2/7) at the time of surgery, with persistent proptosis confirmed by Hertel exophthalmometry. The control sample was obtained from the nasal (medial) fat pad of one subject who underwent upper blepharoplasty for a cosmetic indication. The nasal fat pad was chosen as it shares neural crest origin and molecular characteristics with intraconal orbital fat[17]. The control subject had normal thyroid function, negative thyroid antibodies, normal exophthalmometry measurements, and no history of thyroid or autoimmune disease. 

3.2 Sample preparation and RNA extraction
Orbital adipose tissue samples (approximately 2 ml) were collected from the inferolateral fat compartment during surgery, immediately snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at -80°C until RNA extraction. Total RNA was extracted from tissue samples using RNeasy Lipid tissue mini kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany, cat. No. 74804) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, up to 100 mg tissue was homogenized in QIAzol Lysis Reagent, followed by chloroform phase separation and ethanol precipitation. The aqueous RNA phase was then purified using RNeasy Mini spin columns with successive washes using Buffer RW1 and Buffer RPE. RNA was eluted in RNase-free water after a final dry spin to remove residual ethanol. DNA contamination was removed via DNase treatment. 

3.3 Library preparation, sequencing, and data processing
Purified RNA was randomly fragmented and reverse-transcribed into cDNA. Adapters were ligated to both ends of the cDNA fragments, followed by PCR amplification and size selection to obtain inserts of 200–400 bp. Paired-end sequencing was performed using the Illumina Novaseq platform.
Each biopsy was split in half and processed twice to generate two independent technical-replicate libraries per donor (10 libraries in total). After sequencing, replicate pairs were combined by averaging log₂ fold-change (FC) values and combining p values using Fisher’s method, resulting in one composite expression profile per donor and five biological datasets for downstream analyses.
Raw sequencing reads underwent quality control using FastQC v0.11.7 to assess base quality scores, GC content, and other metrics. Trimming was conducted with Trimmomatic 0.38 to remove adapter sequences, low-quality bases, and short reads. Trimmed reads were aligned to the GRCh38 human reference genome using HISAT2 v2.1.0, which supports spliced read mapping. Transcript assembly was performed with StringTie v2.1.3b, and gene-level expression was quantified as TPM (Transcripts Per Million). All subsequent normalization and visualization procedures were performed using TPM-based expression matrices.

3.4 Differential Expression and Functional Enrichment Analysis
Differential gene expression analysis was performed using a custom Python 3.11 workflow. Technical replicates collapsed as described above. Trimmed Mean of M-values (TMM) normalization was applied to correct for library-size and composition bias. Per-gene statistics were computed as the mean log₂ FC and Fisher-combined p value across TED versus control comparisons. Genes with |log₂ FC| > 1 and p < 0.05 were considered differentially expressed. Volcano plots were capped at –log₁₀(p) = 20, and hierarchical heatmaps were generated for the top 1,000 DEGs ranked by p value. Expression values were log₂-transformed, Z-scored per gene, and clustered using Euclidean distance and complete linkage.
Representative differentially expressed genes were identified from the DEG list by ranking genes according to p value and log₂ FC, followed by manual selection to ensure inclusion of diverse biological categories. Genes representing key functional groups—such as extracellular matrix remodeling, inflammation, stress response, and metabolism—were summarized in Table 1.
Functional enrichment analysis was performed using the Enrichr algorithm implemented in the gseapy (v1.1.0) Python package. Up- and down-regulated genes were tested separately against Gene Ontology (GO) categories—Biological Process (BP), Molecular Function (MF), and Cellular Component (CC) and the KEGG 2021 Human pathway database. Enrichment results were aggregated across all TED samples (T1–T4) using median-based scoring of –log₁₀(p value). Terms with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR adjusted p < 0.05 were considered significant. The top 20 GO and KEGG terms were summarized in tables and visualized as dot plots and summary heatmaps.

3.5 Statistical Analysis
Statistical analyses were performed in R (version 4.2.0), and additional analyses and data visualizations were conducted in Python (version 3.11) using the pandas, NumPy, and matplotlib libraries. Continuous variables were summarized as mean ± standard deviation. For transcriptomic data, differential expression was defined by |log₂ FC| > 1 and p < 0.05 based on Fisher-combined p values across replicates. Multiple-testing correction (FDR) was applied only for functional enrichment analyses. Data handling and figure generation were fully script-based to ensure computational reproducibility.


4. Results
4.1 Sequencing yield and quality assurance
All libraries (one control and four TED samples) passed QC (Supplementary Fig. S1). After adapter and quality trimming, each sample retained 237.2–260.2 million reads (Supplementary Fig. S1A). Phred-quality assessment showed that over 95% of bases in all libraries were Q30 or higher (Supplementary Fig. S1B). Alignment against the hg38 reference generated 98.7–98.8 % uniquely mapped reads for all libraries (Supplementary Fig. S1C). Gene-level sparsity analysis showed that 21,625 of the 46,427 annotated genes (46.6 %) had non-zero counts in all 10 biological libraries, whereas the remaining 24,802 genes displayed at least one zero value and were therefore excluded from downstream analyses (Supplementary Fig. S1D). 

4.2 Global expression profiles across samples 
Box-plots of raw TPM values, log₂-transformed TPM values, and TMM-normalized values show largely overlapping expression distributions for the control and TED samples (Supplementary Fig. S2A). Median log2(TPM + 1) values range from 0.42 to 0.89, and inter-quartile spreads are comparable across libraries, indicating no sample-wide over- or under-expression. The slightly broader upper whisker in T2 reflects a higher fraction of strongly expressed genes; indicating that it represents a genuine biological difference rather than a sequencing artefact. Kernel-density curves for the raw, log2-transformed, and TMM-normalized data (Supplementary Fig. S2B) likewise reveal almost complete overlap among the libraries. Multi-dimensional scaling (MDS) analyses (Supplementary Fig. S2C) show modest separation between control and TED samples but no extreme outliers.

4.3 Differential-expression analysis
The volcano plot in Figure 1 illustrates the differential gene expression profile between the TED and control samples. A total of 3,200 up-regulated and 1,500 down-regulated genes were identified as significantly differentially expressed (p < 0.05, |log₂ FC| > 1). Unsupervised hierarchical clustering of the top 1,000 differentially expressed genes revealed reciprocal expression patterns between the control and TED samples. While clustering did not fully segregate the control from the TED group, the heatmap demonstrated that genes highly expressed in the control (yellow) were generally downregulated in TED (blue), and vice versa, indicating consistent transcriptomic shifts (Fig. 2).
Representative genes with the strongest differential expression are summarized in Table 1. Up-regulated transcripts included several extracellular-matrix–related genes (COMP, COL4A1, FN1, SPP1) and inflammation-associated genes (IL1B, CXCL9, CCL2), together with stress-response genes such as HSPA6. The contractile actin isoform ACTG2 also showed marked up-regulation. Down-regulated genes comprised metabolic and lipid-related enzymes, including HMGCS1, DHCR7, APOC1, GSTM1, ACADL, and CS. These results indicate that TED orbital tissue differs from control tissue by coordinated changes in extracellular-matrix, inflammatory, and metabolic gene expression profiles.

4.4 Functional enrichment analysis
Gene Ontology enrichment analysis revealed that most significant terms belonged to biological processes (Table 2). Upregulated genes in TED were enriched for extracellular matrix organization and inflammatory-response–related pathways (Fig. 3A). Conversely, downregulated genes were largely associated with lipid and sterol biosynthetic processes, reflecting suppression of metabolic activity in TED (Fig. 3B and Table 2). At the cellular-component level, upregulated terms included collagen-containing extracellular matrix and integral component of plasma membrane, whereas no dominant downregulated compartment was identified (Fig. 3C,3D). Within molecular function, enrichment of arachidonate-CoA ligase activity suggested downregulation of lipid-metabolizing enzymes in TED (Fig. 3E,3F).
KEGG pathway analysis revealed marked transcriptional reprogramming in TED, characterized by coordinated activation of inflammatory and adhesion-related pathways alongside suppression of metabolic programs (Table 3). Upregulated genes were significantly enriched in pathways involving extracellular matrix (ECM)–receptor interaction, focal adhesion, cytokine–cytokine receptor signaling, and PI3K–Akt and TNF signaling cascades. In contrast, downregulated pathways were dominated by steroid and fatty-acid biosynthesis, PPAR signaling, and TCA cycle–associated metabolism, reflecting broad inhibition of lipid and energy metabolism. 
5. Discussion
This study utilized bulk RNA-sequencing to characterize the global transcriptional landscape of orbital adipose tissue in patients with clinically inactive TED. Our findings reveal that despite the absence of clinical inflammatory activity (CAS < 3), the orbital microenvironment is far from quiescent; instead, it exhibits active molecular reprogramming characterized by robust ECM remodeling and a profound, coordinated suppression of lipid metabolic pathways. These findings challenge the notion of the inactive phase as a return to homeostatic normalcy, identifying it instead as a distinct state of persistent fibrotic remodeling and metabolic exhaustion.
A defining feature of our inactive TED cohort was the significant upregulation of 3,200 genes, dominated by ECM-related transcripts. Notably, COMP exhibited the most dramatic increase (log2FC = 15.32), alongside robust expression of COL4A1, FN1, and SPP1. This robust pro-fibrotic signature is highly consistent with the single-cell RNA sequencing data of clinically active patients (CAS >3) showing that THY1+ (CD90+) myofibroblast subset highly expressed matrix remodeling-related genes including COL1A1, DCN, MMP2, COL12A1, CTHRC1, and POSTN [15]. Furthermore, the elevated expression of the contractile actin isoform ACTG2 (log2FC = 8.76) and the stress-response gene HSPA6 (log2FC = 8.22) support the hypothesis that OFs in chronic TED maintain a myofibroblast-like phenotype, contributing to irreversible tissue expansion. 
Interestingly, recent single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) data from Kim et al. reported a relative absence of SMA+ myofibroblasts in orbital fat samples with low CAS (< 4), suggesting limited fibrotic activity in clinically stable phases[18]. This discrepancy suggests that molecular fibrotic remodeling may persist at the transcriptomic level even in the apparent absence of classic myofibroblasts. This activity could be driven by transitional cell subsets, such as incompletely differentiated OFs, or representing ongoing remodeling beneath the threshold of clinical detection. 
While our data confirmed a robust fibrotic signature, we observed a profound suppression of metabolic processes in inactive TED orbital fat. We identified a broad downregulation of genes governing lipid biosynthesis and fatty acid metabolism, including HMGCS1, APOC1, ACADL, and PNPLA3. This metabolic shutdown stands in contrast to recent snRNA-seq data from Kim et al.[18], which reported ongoing adipogenic differentiation and elevated expression of PPARG and CEBPB in OFs even in patients with low clinical activity scores (CAS < 4). Given that CD34+ fibrocytes infiltrating the orbit in TED can differentiate into either adipocytes or myofibroblasts depending on environmental cues[11], one possible explanation is that the orbital microenvironment in our cohort—unlike that in the study by Kim et al.—may have preferentially driven these progenitor cells toward a fibrotic fate rather than an adipogenic one. These findings underscore the complexity of chronic TED, suggesting that the inactive phase is not uniform but may oscillate between adipogenic expansion and fibrotic consolidation.
The role of IGF-1R signaling in TED is multifaceted. Kim et al. emphasized the role of the IGF-1 pathway in promoting adipogenesis[18]. However, our observation of elevated PI3K-Akt signaling amidst suppressed adipogenesis suggests an alternative role in the chronic phase. Lu et al. demonstrated that IGF-1R signaling is critical for modulating TSHR-initiated inflammatory responses, such as TNF-α production, in fibrocytes and CD34+ OFs[12]. Although our inactive cohort showed relatively low levels of acute inflammatory cytokines compared to active disease, the sustained PI3K-Akt activity suggests that IGF-1R signaling may shift its function from driving acute inflammation to maintaining fibroblast survival and supporting chronic ECM remodeling as evidenced by our COMP and FN1 upregulation in the late stage.
This interpretation is consistent with the proteomic analysis of extraocular muscles in inactive TED by Wu et al.[19], which reported upregulation of ECM proteins like Versican (VCAN) and cytoskeletal proteins. These findings indicate that extensive tissue remodeling persists across the orbital landscape, encompassing both adipose and muscular compartments—well into the chronic phase. Therefore, the therapeutic efficacy of IGF-1R inhibitors like teprotumumab in chronic TED likely extends beyond simple proptosis reduction via fat volume depletion. It may also involve the interruption of PI3K-Akt-mediated survival signals within myofibroblast-like cells, which otherwise perpetuate tissue stiffness and chronic fibrosis.
This mechanistic framework is corroborated by the recent Phase 4 randomized controlled trial by Douglas et al.[16]. While the persistent proptosis and diplopia of the chronic phase were historically dismissed as irreversible structural sequelae, their observed clinical responsiveness to IGF-1R blockade suggests that the underlying pathological drivers are not biologically dormant. Instead, these clinical outcomes highlight the existence of ongoing, low-grade biological activity—potentially mediated by the PI3K-Akt pathway—that remains amenable to therapeutic modulation even in clinically inactive cases.
While this study provides the global transcriptional landscape of inactive TED, it has important limitations. First, the sample size was small, and the control tissue was derived from a single individual undergoing blepharoplasty; therefore, the generalizability of our findings is limited, and the gene-level statistics should be interpreted as exploratory. Second, bulk RNA sequencing measures average expression across a heterogeneous mixture of cell types, including OFs, adipocytes, endothelial cells, and immune infiltrates, making it difficult to distinguish cell-intrinsic changes from shifts in cell-type composition. Nevertheless, we provide evidence that clinical inactivity does not equate to molecular quiescence. Our results emphasize that chronic TED is a dynamic transcriptomic state, characterized by persistent fibrotic activity and metabolic reprogramming that necessitates long-term molecularly targeted management.
